# Reflection: What the Profiles Taught Us

## Gym Warrior vs Late-Night Study Session

These two profiles sit at opposite poles of the catalog and the system handles both confidently. The Gym Warrior asked for metal, anger, and maximum energy; it got Iron Curtain at 96.2%. The Study Session asked for lofi, calm focus, and low energy; it got Library Rain at 96.5%. Both scores are nearly identical despite the profiles pointing in completely different directions. What that tells you is that when a user's genre and mood exist cleanly in the catalog, the scoring works exactly as intended — the two heaviest features snap onto their targets and pull everything else along with them. The interesting thing is how differently the two lists fall apart after rank 1. The Gym Warrior's second result (Storm Runner at 73.4%) still feels musically connected — rock is metal's close cousin. The Study Session's second result (Midnight Coding at 95.4%) is nearly as strong as the first, because there are three lofi songs in the catalog and they all cluster together. Genre density matters as much as weight design.

## Late-Night Driver vs Soul & Warmth Seeker

Both profiles sit in the middle of the energy range and both get a clear rank-1 winner, but the shape of what follows is completely different. The Driver's top result (Night Drive Loop) scored 97.6% — a near-perfect match on all five features simultaneously, including an exact energy hit. But rank 2 dropped to 56.2% and ranks 3 through 5 were all below 41%. The catalog has one synthwave song and nothing close enough to fill out a meaningful list. The Soul Seeker's top result (Rise Together at 88.6%) is strong but not as dominant, and rank 2 (Velvet Nights at 50.4%) still has genre credit via the r&b/soul close-cousin pair. The Driver's problem is vertical — one perfect match and then a cliff. The Soul Seeker's problem is horizontal — the soul/r&b corner of the catalog just does not have enough songs to serve a user well past position 1. Both are catalog coverage problems, not scoring problems.

## Adversarial Mood Override vs Adversarial Knife Edge

These two profiles expose the same underlying structural issue — the genre weight is not always strong enough to protect a user's stated preference — but they expose it in visibly different ways. The Mood Override is an unambiguous inversion: a reggae fan's top recommendation is an electronic track, and the margin is nearly 10 percentage points. You can see exactly why when you look at the scores: the only reggae song (Island Time) has a mood that scores zero against the user's request, and the mood plus energy gap costs it more points than the genre match can recover. The failure is loud and readable. The Knife Edge is subtler and in some ways more concerning. The top two results show almost identical scores (57.0% vs 56.7%) and both look plausible on paper — until you notice that the rank-1 song is electronic and the rank-2 song is the only classical track in the catalog. A real user would have no way to know from the output that the system essentially flipped a coin between their preferred genre and an entirely unrelated one. The Knife Edge failure is quiet, which makes it harder to catch in production.

## What the Weight Experiment Taught Us

We halved the genre weight (3.0 to 1.5), doubled the energy weight (2.0 to 4.0), and updated the normalizer to 11.0. Every single rank-1 result stayed the same across all seven profiles. That result is easy to misread as good news — the system is stable under perturbation — but the more accurate reading is that the standard profiles are so well-served by the catalog that almost any reasonable weight configuration produces the same top result. What the experiment actually revealed is where the weight design is not the problem. Both adversarial failures got worse when energy was amplified: the Mood Override winner's score rose from 66.6% to 78.2%, and the Knife Edge margin between the electronic song and the classical song widened from 0.3 points to roughly 12 points. Increasing energy power handed even more scoring authority to Overdrive Protocol, which is already a free rider. The lesson is that the adversarial failures are baked into the catalog's structure — isolated mood nodes and single-song genres — and no weight adjustment rescues them. Fixing these profiles requires either expanding the catalog or adding neighborhood connections for the orphaned genres and moods.
